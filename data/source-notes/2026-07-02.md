### Disallowed Croatia Goal (113th minute) — Snicko/CBT Technology

- Pasalic was clearly offside; if Matanovic hadn't touched it, the goal stands; if he did, it's offside *(Men in Blazers, Rory Smith)*
- Rory: "The spin on the ball did not change at all... he touched it, I'm confident that's slightly correct. But the faintest of touches, and the fact that it doesn't alter the flight of the ball to me matters. Technically is offside" *(Men in Blazers, Rory Smith)*
- Betty Clever: "This was the first time Snicko technology, which cricket fans will know all about, has been used in the knockouts" *(Men in Blazers, Betty Clever)*
- Betty: "Croatia were eliminated from the tournament on the brush of a ball over the side of a player's head, which was replayed 5,000 times pitchside by a very tired referee" *(Men in Blazers, Betty Clever)*

## Offside Controversy Context (from The Athletic, published Jul 4)

### Connected Ball Technology (CBT) / Snicko Explained
- The Adidas Trionda ball contains a 500Hz inertial measurement unit (IMU) motion sensor
- Combined with player position tracking, CBT delivers data at 500 times per second to determine ball touches
- Semi-automated offside technology (SAOT) captures 100 frames per second using 10,000 data points on all 22 players
- The Croatia-Portugal disallowed goal: CBT showed a spike on the sensor indicating Matanovic touched the ball, making Pasalic offside
- Arsene Wenger (head of global development) pushing "daylight offside" — player ruled onside if any body part in line with last defender, rather than behind
- Canadian Premier League piloting daylight offside; retrospectively assessed all tournament games — concluded 17 additional goals would have been scored under their interpretation
- IFAB composition: England, Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland each get one vote; all other 207 associations collectively get four votes through the governing body; three-quarter majority needed to change laws
